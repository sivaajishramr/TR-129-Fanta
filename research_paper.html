Abstract

The Rights of Persons with Disabilities (RPWD) Act, 2016, mandates universal accessibility across all public infrastructure in India. Despite this, a systematic audit of bus stops and transit terminals reveals alarming non-compliance rates. This paper presents AccessAudit AI, an end-to-end intelligent platform that audits the accessibility compliance of 249 public transit stops across 36 districts of Tamil Nadu. The system employs a multi-modal AI pipeline combining Natural Language Processing (NLP) for grievance analysis, image-based computer vision for photo/video audits, a weighted gap scoring engine for quantitative compliance measurement, and an automated contractor accountability system. Our analysis of 317 citizen grievances and 12 accessibility parameters reveals a statewide average gap score of 78.4%, with 69% of stops rated as “Critical.” The platform enables real-time monitoring, automated complaint filing, and data-driven contractor ranking — transforming passive disability rights enforcement into an active, AI-powered compliance ecosystem.

Keywords: Accessibility Auditing, RPWD Act 2016, NLP Grievance Classification, Computer Vision, Public Transport, Disability Rights, Tamil Nadu, AI for Social Good, SDG 11

1. Introduction

India's Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act, 2016, requires all public buildings and transport infrastructure to be fully accessible within a defined timeline. Section 40–46 of the Act specifically mandates ramps, tactile paths, Braille signage, audio announcement systems, accessible toilets, and trained staff at public transit facilities. However, ground-level enforcement remains weak due to the absence of systematic, scalable auditing tools.

Tamil Nadu operates one of India's largest state transport networks, with thousands of bus stops serving millions of daily commuters. Our field research across Tiruchirappalli district — later expanded to 36 districts statewide — reveals that only 8.8% of bus stops meet basic accessibility standards, leaving persons with disabilities (PwDs) systematically excluded from public transport.

This paper introduces AccessAudit AI, a web-based platform that addresses this gap through four interconnected AI-driven modules: (1) a quantitative gap scoring engine with weighted accessibility parameters, (2) NLP-powered grievance classification using TF-IDF vectorization and K-Means clustering, (3) a photo/video-based audit system using image analysis, and (4) an automated contractor accountability and alert system.

2. Related Work

Previous approaches to accessibility auditing have relied primarily on manual surveys and checklist-based inspections. Bezyak et al. (2020) highlight that manual methods are costly, slow, and geographically constrained. Digital accessibility tools like Google's Accessibility Scanner and WAVE focus on web/app interfaces rather than physical infrastructure.

In the Indian context, the Accessible India Campaign (Sugamya Bharat Abhiyan) launched in 2015 set targets for public infrastructure accessibility but lacked real-time monitoring tools. Rao & Sharma (2021) proposed sensor-based monitoring for railway stations, but their approach requires costly hardware installation. Our work differentiates itself by using a purely software-based, data-driven approach that requires no hardware deployment — only a web browser and existing grievance data.

The system is built on a Flask (Python) backend with a vanilla HTML/CSS/JS frontend. All data is stored in structured JSON files covering 249 transit stops with 12 accessibility parameters each, plus 317 citizen grievances. The architecture follows a modular design:

3.1 Weighted Gap Scoring Engine

Each stop is evaluated against 12 accessibility criteria derived from the RPWD Act 2016 and the Harmonised Guidelines issued by the Ministry of Urban Development. Each criterion carries a weight reflecting its impact on PwD mobility:

Feature Weight Legal Reference
Wheelchair Ramp 15 RPWD Act §40, NBC 2016
Tactile Path (TGSI) 12 IS 14489, NBC 2016
Audio Announcement 10 RPWD Act §41
Wheelchair Waiting Area 10 RPWD Act §40
Braille Signage 12 RPWD Act §42, IS 14489
Elevator / Lift 8 NBC 2016 Part 3
Accessible Toilet 18 RPWD Act §40, Swachh Bharat
Trained Staff Assistance 15 RPWD Act §43

The Gap Score for stop i is computed as:

Gapi = (Σ wmissing / Σ wtotal) × 100%

The overall Priority Score combines gap score (50%), normalized grievance density (30%), and daily footfall (20%), enabling resource allocation to stops where impact is greatest.

3.2 NLP Grievance Classification

Citizen grievances are processed through a three-stage NLP pipeline: (1) text preprocessing with stopword removal and lemmatization, (2) TF-IDF vectorization to convert text to numerical features, and (3) K-Means clustering with k=6 to classify grievances into predefined accessibility categories: Infrastructure, Toilet Facilities, Signage & Braille, Audio/Visual Aids, Staff Assistance, and Ramp/Wheelchair access.

The system achieves a silhouette score of 0.31, indicating meaningful cluster separation. Each grievance is further classified into sub-problems with fault attribution (contractor vs. civilian responsibility) and priority assignment (High/Medium/Low).

3.3 Photo/Video Audit Module

Users can upload photographs or videos of bus stops for automated accessibility assessment. For video uploads, a key frame is extracted at the 2-second mark using HTML5 Canvas API. The image is analyzed server-side using Python Pillow for feature detection — evaluating structural elements (ramps, tactile paths), signage presence (Braille, LED boards), and facility availability (toilets, seating).

Audit results automatically populate a complaint filing form with detected missing features, enabling users to file formal RPWD Act complaints with auto-assigned contractor responsibility and unique reference tracking IDs.

3.4 Contractor Accountability & Alert System

The platform maps six real Tiruchirappalli-based government contractors (e.g., M/s. Jyothi Constructions, S.R. Vedhaah, Larsen & Toubro) to their respective accessibility domains. An AI-driven leaderboard ranks contractors by performance score computed from grievance volume, severity distribution, geographic spread, and fault attribution. A real-time alert system monitors all 249 stops and generates three tiers of notifications:

  • Critical (🔴): Stops with gap score > 85% — immediate intervention required
  • Warning (🟡): Stops with ≥3 grievance complaints — monitoring recommended
  • Info (🔵): Stops with audits overdue by >180 days — re-assessment needed

The results reveal a systemic accessibility crisis in Tamil Nadu's public transport infrastructure. With an average gap score of 78.4%, the state is significantly non-compliant with RPWD Act mandates. The most commonly missing features are tactile paths for visually impaired users (89% of stops), Braille signage (91%), and audio announcement systems (85%). Only stops with major renovations in the past 2 years (e.g., Chatram Bus Stand, Trichy Junction) achieve acceptable compliance levels.

The district-level analysis enabled by our statewide expansion reveals significant inter-district variance: larger districts like Chennai and Coimbatore show marginally better compliance due to Smart City funding, while smaller districts like Ariyalur and Perambalur show near-total non-compliance (gap scores > 90%).

5. Alignment with Sustainable Development Goals

AccessAudit AI directly addresses SDG 11: Sustainable Cities and Communities (Target 11.2 — affordable and accessible transport systems for all, with special attention to PwDs) and SDG 10: Reduced Inequalities (Target 10.2 — social, economic and political inclusion of all). The platform transforms disability rights monitoring from a reactive complaint-based model to a proactive, AI-driven continuous auditing system.

6. Conclusion and Future Work

AccessAudit AI demonstrates that AI-powered accessibility auditing is technically feasible, scalable, and impactful. By combining weighted gap scoring, NLP-based grievance analysis, photo/video-based auditing, and automated contractor accountability, the platform provides a comprehensive compliance monitoring solution that could be deployed by the Tamil Nadu State Transport Corporation (TNSTC) or the Department of Social Welfare.

Future work includes: (1) integration with pre-trained object detection models (YOLO/MobileNet) for more accurate photo audit feature detection, (2) multilingual support for Tamil-language grievances, (3) a Progressive Web App for on-site mobile auditing, (4) voice-based complaint filing for visually impaired users using the Web Speech API, and (5) integration with government e-governance portals for automated compliance reporting.
